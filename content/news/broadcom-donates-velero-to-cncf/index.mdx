Broadcom has given Velero to the CNCF. The Sandbox application was filed in February, and the acceptance was announced at KubeCon EU in Amsterdam last week. After years living under VMware's roof, Kubernetes' most widely adopted backup tool now has a vendor-neutral home.

This matters because of who's doing the giving. Broadcom's acquisition of VMware left a lot of open source communities nervous about the future of projects they depended on. Donating Velero to the CNCF is a concrete signal that Broadcom isn't pulling the ladder up behind it. Chris Aniszczyk, CNCF's CTO, framed it as fostering "community collaboration and growth," but the subtext is clearer: Broadcom wants to be seen as a good steward.

For platform teams running stateful workloads, Velero is probably already in your cluster. It handles backup, restore, and migration of both cluster state and persistent volumes. If you've ever had to recover from a botched Helm upgrade at 2am, there's a decent chance Velero was involved.

The project isn't arriving at CNCF as a single-vendor effort. Nikhita Raghunath at Broadcom filed the Sandbox application, but maintainers already span Broadcom, Red Hat, and Microsoft. The existing governance model follows CNCF principles: consensus-based decisions, supermajority voting, and consolidated company voting to prevent any one vendor from dominating.

What changes practically? Not much right now. Your Helm charts still work. Your backup schedules don't break. The Sandbox designation is the first rung on the CNCF ladder, with Incubating and Graduated status ahead. The real shift is structural: contribution barriers drop when a project isn't perceived as someone else's product.

Velero started life as Heptio Ark back in 2017. VMware acquired Heptio, then Broadcom acquired VMware. That's two acquisitions deep for a tool that thousands of teams depend on daily. Every ownership change made the community hold its breath. This donation finally removes that uncertainty.

Broadcom also shipped VKS 3.6 alongside this announcement, adding Kubernetes 1.35 support with 24-month extended version support, RHEL 9 compatibility, and declarative TuneD profiles for kernel tuning. They're positioning themselves as one of the top five long-term contributors to CNCF projects.

If you're already running Velero, keep going. If you've been holding off on contributing upstream because of the VMware/Broadcom uncertainty, that blocker is gone.
